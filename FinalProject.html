7 WONDERS OF THE WORLD

ABOUT

The amazing works of art and architecture known as the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World serve as a testament to the ingenuity, imagination and sheer hard work of which human beings are capable. They are also, however, reminders of the human capacity for disagreement, destruction and, possibly, embellishment. As soon as ancient writers compiled a list of “seven wonders,” it became fodder for debate over which achievements deserved inclusion. The original list comes from a work by Philo of Byzantium written in 225 B.C. called On The Seven Wonders. Ultimately, human hands joined with natural forces to destroy all but one of the wonders. Furthermore, it is possible that at least one of the wonders might not have existed at all. Still, all seven continue to inspire and be celebrated as the remarkable products of the creativity and skill of Earth’s early civilizations.

THE COLOSEUM OF ROME

The Colosseum, Rome, Italy

As one of the few mostly intact structures from the Roman Empire, the Colosseum stands today as a monument to ancient Rome’s architectural and engineering prowess. It is also a major source of tourism revenue for the Italian government. In 2018 the Colosseum, Roman Forum, and Palatine Hill together brought in more than $63.3 million (€53.8 million), the highest revenue of any tourist attraction in Italy.

THE GREAT WALL OF CHINA

The Great Wall of China

Tourism was regarded as a marginal economic sector in China until the early 1980's, with organized circuits few in number and accommodation limited (Courtin, 1993). Tourism revenue grew by 17.53% annually in 1980-88 (Wang, 1993), and although it dropped in 1989 due to political events in Beijing it grew again during the 1990's. Tourism is now a major part of the Chinese economy, with the country expecting a total of 36.8 million tourists from Hong Kong, Macao, Taiwan and abroad to visit and stay overnight in China during 2004. Overall income from the tourism sector this year is predicted to reach 560 billion yuan (US$67.7 billion), of which US$20.5 billion will come from international tourists, according to a plan released on 8 January 2004 by He Guangwei, general director of the National Tourism Administration. There are now about 9,000 travel agencies and over 33 million tourism employees in China.

CHRIST THE REDEEMER BRAZIL

Christ the Redeemer, Brazil

The Christ the Redeemer statue is probably the most famous monument in Brazil. It has an average of 5.500 visitors a day, with a record of 20 thousand visitors during World Youth Day in 2013. The statue is located in Rio de Janeiro and generates R$1.5 billion of profit per year.

MACHU PICCU PERU

Machu Picchu, Peru

Machu Picchu attracts hundreds of thousands of tourists every year. It is the most visited site in South America, generating a reported $40 million each year for the economy of Peru.
